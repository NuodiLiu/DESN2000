This project aims to design a shared renting and co-living assistance application for students in Sydney. Many students have a limited budget for accommodation, while the rent in Sydney is relatively high. For example, the rent for a one-bedroom apartment can range from around $800 to $1,150 per week, while a two-bedroom apartment can range from around $1,200 to $1,650 per week.[1] As a result, it is often too expensive for students to live alone, especially international students who may be affected by currency exchange rates or students who rely on student loans. Therefore, many students need to find suitable housemates to reduce rent and utility costs. However, they may face challenges such as cultural differences, communication barriers, trust issues, scams, and incompatible living habits. [2]

To understand these problems, we designed a survey for students from different countries and regions. We collected responses from 23 students, including students from Bangladesh, India, China, the Philippines, and Indonesia. Most respondents were international students with prior or current shared-renting experience in Sydney. The survey asked about their cultural background, education level, English ability, length of stay in Australia, expectations of housemates, and dissatisfaction with existing renting applications. AI-assisted tools were used to group open-ended survey responses into themes such as trust, cultural compatibility, English communication, lifestyle habits, and dissatisfaction with existing platforms. The AI-generated categories were then checked against the original responses to avoid overgeneralisation.

Based on the survey analysis, we found that some students prefer housemates from similar cultural or linguistic backgrounds. They explained that living with housemates from very different cultures may require more time to explain daily habits, behaviours, and expectations, which can increase communication difficulties. For students who do not mind living with people from different countries, English ability is still an important factor. Since English is often the only shared language between housemates, students may feel uncomfortable living with someone whose English level is too low for daily communication or necessary conversations about rent, bills, chores, and house rules.

The survey also showed that existing rental applications do not fully meet students’ needs. Platforms such as Realestate and Domain mainly focus on property rental itself and do not provide enough support for finding compatible housemates. These platforms also do not allow users to clearly express their expectations for potential housemates. Flatmates can help users find housemates, but the identity of potential housemates or landlords may not always be clearly verified, which can create risks of scams or harassment. Other platforms, such as Little Red Book, Instagram, and Facebook, allow users to discuss renting together and communicate informally. However, because these are social media platforms rather than dedicated rental services, it can be difficult to verify the housing condition, landlord identity, maintenance responsibilities, and tenant protections.

In conclusion, our research shows that students have different needs depending on their culture, study schedule, English communication ability, and experience living in Australia. Therefore, a useful shared renting application should not only help students find housemates, but also support identity verification, safe communication, lifestyle matching, co-living rules, rent and utility bill splitting, and housework management.

Based on this background, our application is designed to help students find trustworthy and compatible housemates while managing shared living more clearly, safely, and fairly.
