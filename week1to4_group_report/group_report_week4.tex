\section{Introduction and Project Context}

This interim report examines shared accommodation problems experienced by university students, with attention to international students and people entering a shared house for the first time. The project brief frames shared living as a practical and social challenge that affects everyday routines, trust and wellbeing. Early research suggested that many later problems begin before move in, when students judge a listing, contact potential housemates and decide whether the arrangement feels trustworthy and compatible. For this reason, the report focuses on how students evaluate accommodation and housemates before they commit.

\boxfigure{Project focus within the Smart Share House problem space}{The wider project considers everyday shared living. This report narrows the focus to the decision period before commitment, when students need to judge listing trust, housemate fit and early expectations.}{figfocus}

International students and existing housemates were prioritised because both groups carry substantial risk at the point of commitment. The team used primary users for persona and journey mapping, while secondary and affected stakeholders informed the constraints and risks.

\section{Research Methods and Synthesis}

The team used mixed methods to identify common patterns and understand why those patterns matter in everyday shared living. Surveys showed the scale of recurring concerns, while the interview and persona work gave context to the decisions students make when choosing accommodation or housemates.

The research questions examined how students judge whether a listing can be trusted, what they need to know before contacting a landlord or housemate, which qualities shape housemate fit and how shared responsibilities can create conflict after move in.

\section{Key Research Findings}

\subsection{Trust and Safety Shape Accommodation Decisions}

All 16 international student survey respondents reported concern about rental scams, with half being very concerned and half being slightly concerned. This suggests that students do not only need access to more listings. They need help checking the person who posted a listing, matching the photos with the room and keeping early messages inside a safer platform. Student feedback should also be easy to find.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.94\textwidth]{survey_scams_concern.png}
    \caption{International student survey result. Concern about rental scams, n=16.}
    \label{figscams}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Housemate Compatibility Is a High Priority Decision Factor}

The international student survey showed an average roommate compatibility importance score of 4.44 out of 5. The interview also showed that compatibility is practical rather than abstract. Students wanted to know whether housemates keep spaces clean, respect privacy and follow routines that would not clash with their own. The design should make this information visible before users commit to a room.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.94\textwidth]{survey_roommate_compatibility.png}
    \caption{International student survey result. Roommate compatibility importance, n=16.}
    \label{figcompatibility}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Communication and Information Gaps Increase Uncertainty}

International students may not know Australian rental processes, inspection expectations or the cues used to judge whether a listing is reliable. The interview participant wanted to see where the room was, what it looked like in realistic photos, whether reviews were available and what facilities were nearby. This points to a need for listing pages and messages that guide the user through a decision rather than leaving them to interpret scattered information on their own.

\begin{figure}[H]

\end{figure}

\subsection{Shared Living Conflicts Often Come From Unclear Expectations}

Domestic student findings showed that informal household systems can fail after move in. All domestic respondents relied on verbal agreements for household work, and all had experienced conflict over household responsibilities. Bill splitting was also weakly structured, as 66.67\% reported no formal arrangement and all reported late payment issues. The design should help students discuss responsibility expectations before move in, rather than relying only on personality matching.

\section{Personas, Scenarios and Journey Maps}

The personas were built from repeated survey and interview themes rather than assumptions. Each persona includes a scenario and a journey map so the team can see what the user is trying to do, what pressure they feel and where support is missing. The journey maps showed that the main breakdown occurs while users shortlist options, make contact and check compatibility, because they must make consequential decisions with limited trust and lifestyle information.

\section{Problem Definition}

\subsection{Convergence From Research}

The team first explored trust in rentals, how housemates communicate, how household responsibilities are shared and how conflict develops. Survey and interview findings showed that many of these concerns connect to an earlier decision point. Students often commit to accommodation or housemates before they have enough reliable information about listing legitimacy, lifestyle fit and responsibility expectations. The team therefore focused on the stage where students evaluate accommodation and housemates before commitment.

\boxfigure{Convergence diagram linking research themes to final problem definition}{Trust concerns, housemate fit, communication barriers and responsibility expectations meet at the same decision point. Students need better support while evaluating accommodation and housemates before commitment.}{figconvergence}

\subsection{Final Problem Statement}

International students and new shared house residents need a trustworthy and structured way to evaluate accommodation and potential housemates before committing. Existing search and communication processes make it difficult to check whether a listing is legitimate, compare lifestyle fit and discuss who cleans, how bills are paid, how privacy is handled and what the household expects.

\subsection{Justification}

This problem matters because it appears before users make a risky housing decision and then shapes their living experience. The research showed that trust concerns, unclear compatibility and informal expectations can lead to anxiety, poor decisions and conflict after move in. A focused problem definition gives the next design phase a clear direction. The solution should let users check whether a listing can be trusted, understand how a potential housemate lives, communicate through a guided flow and agree on expectations before moving in.

\section{Conclusion}

This report demonstrates the Empathise and Define stages of the project. The research evidence led the team to a specific problem. Students need stronger support before they commit to a room or housemate. The next design phase should test concepts that help users check trust, understand compatibility and agree on shared living expectations before move in.
