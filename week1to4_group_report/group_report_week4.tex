\section{Introduction and Project Context}

This interim report explores shared accommodation challenges faced by university students, with a focus on international students and new share-house residents. The broader project brief identifies chores, expenses, communication, maintenance, privacy, wellbeing and conflict as key shared-living issues. Our early research showed that many later shared-living conflicts begin before move-in, when students judge listings, contact potential housemates and decide whether the arrangement is trustworthy and compatible. Therefore, this report focuses on the pre-commitment accommodation and housemate evaluation experience.

\boxfigure{Project focus within the Smart Share-House problem space}{Broad shared-living problem space: chores, expenses, communication, maintenance, privacy and wellbeing. This report's focus: pre-commitment listing trust, housemate compatibility and early shared-living expectations.}{fig:focus}

International students and existing housemates were prioritised because they experience the highest risk at the commitment point: international students must judge unfamiliar listings and rental norms, while existing housemates live with the long-term consequences of poor matching. Primary users were selected for full persona and journey mapping, while secondary and affected stakeholders were used to define constraints and risks.

\section{Research Methods and Synthesis}

The team used a mixed research approach to collect both broad patterns and deeper explanations. Surveys captured common concerns, while interview and persona work helped explain why those concerns matter in everyday shared living.

The research questions focused on four decision-critical areas: how students judge listing trustworthiness, what information they need before contacting landlords or housemates, what qualities make a housemate compatible, and what shared-living responsibilities commonly create conflict after move-in.

\section{Key Research Findings}

\subsection{Trust and Safety Shape Accommodation Decisions}

All 16 international student survey respondents reported concern about rental scams, with half being very concerned and half slightly concerned. This indicates that students do not only need more listings; they need signals that help them judge whether listings, landlords and housemates are genuine. This creates a design requirement for verified listings, realistic photos, reviews and safer in-platform communication.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.94\textwidth]{survey_scams_concern.png}
    \caption{International student survey result: concern about rental scams, n=16.}
    \label{fig:scams}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Housemate Compatibility Is a High-Priority Decision Factor}

The international student survey showed an average roommate compatibility importance score of 4.44 out of 5. Interview findings also highlighted cleanliness, respect, privacy and daily routines as important compatibility factors. This creates a design requirement for visible compatibility information before users commit to a room.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.94\textwidth]{survey_roommate_compatibility.png}
    \caption{International student survey result: roommate compatibility importance, n=16.}
    \label{fig:compatibility}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Communication and Information Gaps Increase Uncertainty}

International students may be unfamiliar with Australian rental processes, inspection expectations and how to judge listing reliability. The interview participant also expected clear location details, realistic photos, reviews and surrounding facility information before deciding. This creates a design requirement for structured listing information and guided communication rather than unstructured social media-style messaging.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering

    \caption{International student survey result: accommodation search challenges before arrival, n=16.}

\subsection{Shared-Living Conflicts Often Come From Unclear Expectations}

Domestic student findings showed that informal household systems can break down after move-in. All domestic respondents used verbal agreements for chores, and all reported conflict over household responsibilities. Bill splitting was also weakly structured, with 66.67\% reporting no formal arrangement and all reporting issues with late payment. This creates a design requirement for clarifying expectations around chores, bills and shared rules before move-in, not only personality matching.

\section{Problem Definition}

\subsection{Convergence From Research}

The team initially explored several shared-living problem areas: rental trust, communication, chores, bills, privacy, maintenance and household conflict. Survey and interview findings showed that many of these issues are connected by one earlier decision point: students often commit to accommodation or housemates before they have enough reliable information about listing legitimacy, lifestyle compatibility and shared responsibilities. Therefore, the team converged on the pre-commitment accommodation and housemate evaluation stage as the most important problem situation to address.

\boxfigure{Convergence diagram linking research themes to final problem definition}{Trust and safety + housemate compatibility + communication barriers + shared-responsibility expectations converge on one high-risk moment: evaluating accommodation and housemates before commitment.}{fig:convergence}

\subsection{Final Problem Statement}

\begin{quote}
International students and new share-house residents need a trustworthy and structured way to evaluate accommodation and potential housemates before committing, because current search and communication processes make it difficult to verify listing legitimacy, compare lifestyle compatibility, and clarify shared responsibilities such as cleaning, bills, privacy and household expectations.
\end{quote}

\subsection{Justification}

This problem matters because it occurs before users make a high-risk housing decision and directly affects later shared-living outcomes. Research findings showed that trust concerns, unclear compatibility and informal household expectations can lead to anxiety, poor decisions and conflict after move-in. A focused problem definition also gives the next design phase clear requirements: the solution should support trust verification, compatibility comparison, structured communication and expectation-setting.

\section{Conclusion}

This report demonstrates the Empathise and Define stages of the project. The research evidence converged on a specific problem: students need stronger support before committing to a room or housemate. The next design phase should test concepts that help users verify trust, compare compatibility and agree on shared-living expectations before move-in.
